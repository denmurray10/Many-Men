Men Matter

It’s Not Weak to Speak – It’s
How Recovery Starts.

When men get the right support, things can change quickly for the better. Talking, learning new tools and feeling backed by others helps many men feel more in control, less alone and more hopeful about the future, so they can show up more fully at home, at work and in themselves.

Reaching out for support is a sign of strength, not weakness, and you don’t have to do this on your own.

Key numbers for men in the UK

12.5%

Of men in England have a diagnosed mental health disorder, and surveys suggest many more experience symptoms but never seek support.

1 in 8

Men are thought to be living with a common mental health problem such as depression or anxiety at any one time.

75%

Of people who die by suicide in the UK are men, and suicide is the single leading cause of death for men under 50.
Changes in Mood

Increased irritability, anger, aggression, or a “short fuse,” especially if this is out of character.

Pulling Away

Pulling away from friends, family, and usual activities, wanting to be left alone more often.

Body Changes

Noticeable changes in appetite, weight, energy levels, or unexplained physical aches and pains.

What can you do to help?

Donate

Your donation, however big or small, will help us achieve good mental health for all - not just for some.

Participate

Our Personal Experience Network, OPEN, is a diverse online community of people who share their experience to inform what we do.

Fundraising

Explore the range of active challenges available across the UK. Get fit and raise money for the Mental Health Foundation, at the same time.

Donating, fundraising, and taking part in events can make a real difference to men’s mental health and wellbeing because they create purpose, connection, and hope. These activities help men feel useful rather than like a “burden,” and that shift alone can ease feelings of isolation, low mood, and anxiety. When a man chooses to support a mental health cause, he is not just helping others; he is also giving himself chances to connect, move his body, and talk about things he might otherwise keep hidden.​

You Don’t Have to Do This Alone

You Don’t Have to Do This Alone means there is always someone who can listen, support, and guide you through what you’re facing. Reaching out is not a sign of weakness; it is a sign that you are taking your mental health seriously and giving yourself a chance to feel better. You deserve support just as much as anyone else, whether you are feeling low, stressed, numb, angry, or simply “not yourself.”

Reading honest experiences from others can make it easier to recognise your own feelings, feel less alone, and believe that change is possible.

Daniel Morris, 36
“For years I put on a mask and told everyone I was ‘fine’ while silently battling anxiety, anger, and late-night drinking that was getting out of hand. I kept telling myself to man up, and it got to the point where I was snapping at my kids and dreading going to work. One night I searched online for men’s mental health support that didn’t feel clinical or judgmental and came across Many Men. The language was simple, honest, and it sounded like it actually understood what blokes go through.

I started by reading the short guides and trying some of the breathing and grounding techniques before bed, and for the first time in years I felt like my head was quietening down a bit. I then used the site to find local support and booked an appointment with my GP, something I’d put off for years. Many Men helped me realise I didn’t have to do this alone, and step by step I’ve gone from just surviving the day to actually enjoying time with my family and making plans for the future. It didn’t fix everything overnight, but it gave me the push, tools, and confidence I needed to turn my life around.”
Mark Jensen, 42
“For most of my thirties I buried stress under long hours, takeaway, and scrolling on my phone until 2am, telling myself this was just what being a man and a dad looked like. Inside I felt numb, exhausted, and disconnected from everyone around me. I found Many Men after seeing a link in a friend’s social media post about men’s mental health and clicked it late one night when I couldn’t sleep. The site felt calm and straightforward, not preachy, and it was the first place that explained what I was feeling in a way that actually made sense to me.

I started using some of the quick techniques on my lunch break and before bed, and slowly the constant tightness in my chest began to ease. The guidance on how to talk to my GP gave me the words I didn’t have, and I finally booked an appointment instead of cancelling again. With support, and by coming back to Many Men’s tools when things feel heavy, I’ve gone from barely getting through the week to actually having energy to play with my kids and make time for myself. It showed me that asking for help is not weakness but the first step back to feeling like myself again.”
Aaron Phillps, 41
“I was quietly falling apart after a breakup and money problems, but on the outside nothing looked wrong. I stopped seeing friends, stopped going to the gym, and spent most nights lying awake thinking everyone else had life figured out except me. I came across Many Men while looking for practical advice for men with anxiety and clicked because it didn’t feel like a typical clinical site. The way it spoke directly to men who bottle things up made me feel seen rather than judged.

I tried some of the simple breathing and grounding exercises during panic moments and they helped me get through work days that used to feel impossible. Reading other men’s stories and the clear steps on where to get help gave me the courage to tell a close friend what was really going on and to reach out for counselling. Over time, I’ve rebuilt routines, gone back to exercising, and I’m no longer ashamed to say when I’m struggling. Many Men turned a desperate late-night search into the starting point of getting my confidence and hope back.”
Chris O'Neil, 31
"After being made redundant, I felt like I’d lost my purpose overnight. I told my family I was fine and that I’d find something quickly, but weeks turned into months and I slipped into heavy drinking and long days in bed. One afternoon, my daughter sent me a link to Many Men and said I might find it useful. At first I was defensive, but I opened it later that night and was surprised to see a space that spoke honestly about the pressure men feel to always be strong.

I read through the guidance on low mood and finances, and used the suggested steps to contact my GP and a local support service. The small daily techniques on the site helped me get out of the house, organise my day, and slowly cut down on alcohol. I’m now volunteering part-time, rebuilding my confidence, and talking more openly with my family instead of shutting down. Many Men helped me realise that my worth isn’t tied to my job title and that there is a way back, even when everything feels like it’s fallen apart."
James Richardson, 27
“I grew up thinking the only acceptable emotions for a man were anger or silence, so when I started feeling low, overwhelmed, and constantly on edge at university, I had no idea what to do with it. I snapped at people, skipped lectures, and pretended everything was fine in group chats. I found Many Men through a mental health link on my university’s website and clicked because it mentioned support specifically for men. The tone felt real and straightforward, like someone talking to me, not at me.

I used the short-term coping tools before exams and on days when getting out of bed felt impossible, and they helped bring everything down from a 10 to something I could manage. The clear signposting to helplines and online support meant I could chat to someone anonymously at first, which made asking for help less scary. Over time, I’ve told a couple of close mates what I’ve been going through and built healthier routines around sleep, exercise, and study. Many Men gave me the permission and practical steps I needed to take my mental health seriously instead of pretending I was okay.”
